How I was introduced to
america ?

America came to me first as a distant dream, slowly turning into a journey I would one day live for real.

I still remember the first time America entered my world—not through a plane window or a stamped passport, but through stories, flickering screens, and a quiet curiosity that refused to fade. Growing up, my earliest impressions were shaped by films like Forrest Gump and Home Alone, where wide suburban streets, yellow school buses, and towering city skylines felt almost unreal. America, to me, wasn't just a country; it was an idea—something vast, distant, and full of motion.

As I got older, that idea began to take shape. I followed the footsteps of dreamers and innovators, reading about figures like Steve Jobs and Elon Musk, who seemed to embody a culture where ambition wasn't just accepted—it was expected. Through music, too, I felt a connection. Songs by Taylor Swift and Eminem carried stories of youth, struggle, and identity that somehow resonated across continents.

But it wasn't until I finally set foot in America that everything shifted. My introduction became real the moment I landed in New York City. The air felt different—charged, almost electric. Walking through Times Square at night, surrounded by neon lights and a sea of strangers, I realized this was the same place I had only ever seen on screens. Yet being there, hearing the languages, feeling the pace, it was far more overwhelming and alive than I had imagined.

From there, my journey unfolded like a story I had been unknowingly preparing for all my life. I stood at the edge of the Grand Canyon, where silence stretched wider than any skyline. I wandered the streets of San Francisco, where hills rolled into the horizon and innovation seemed to linger in the air. Each place added a new layer to my understanding—America wasn't just one thing. It was contradictions, diversity, and constant reinvention.

Looking back, I realize I wasn't introduced to America in a single moment. It was a slow unfolding—a mix of imagination, culture, and eventually, experience. And even now, I feel like I'm still being introduced, one journey at a time.
